Flexbox

Evaluating Demand Response Potential in Nicaragua

Find Out More

Overview

Nicaragua is rapidly transitioning to low carbon electricity generation – primarily with wind power – but it still requires bunker fuel oil generators to meet demand for electricity at peak times (and when electricity is most expensive during the day), to balance variability from wind generators. Oil is one of the most expensive fuels for electricity generation and relying on it comes at great expense to the country, as it affects economic efficiency, energy security, and the budgets of households and small businesses throughout the country.

This project explored a participatory approach to a low-carbon power transition by developing tools to shift the timing of electricity consumption from residential and commercial loads to periods of abundant electricity supply. Using existing ‘off-the-shelf’ technology and flexible demand concepts which have been amply explored in the literature, the FlexBox project piloted what we believe to be the first co-implementation of behavioral energy efficiency and demand response in Latin America. 60 households and micro-enterprises were recruited to participate in the program, and 30 of them received a sensor gateway called the FlexBox and a suite of energy information products that could help them manage their consumption (paper energy reports, and SMS). The remaining 30 participants were used as a control and received nothing.

The project lasted from January 2015 (baseline) to December 2016 (endline). At the end of the study, participants in the treatment group had reduced their energy consumption by 8% due to their own individual energy management, and had reduced 83 Watt-hours per peak hour price event of flexible demand (on average). At scale (2 million people, or a third of the population engaging in behavioral energy efficiency), the country could save $US 29 million a year using average market prices, and $US 7 million if only 8 percent of the people participated (or 500,000 people). Flexible demand at scale could, on the other hand, save $US 18 million every year (using peak prices), and save $US 5 million if only a small fraction of the population were participating.

The FlexBox project was implemented in Managua (Nicaragua) as a joint collaboration between researchers from the University of California Berkeley, Nicaragua’s National Engineering University, with the lead technology integration, development and implementation being performed by Kimera Systems (formerly Niuera). Local implementation and logistics management was performed by Kimera Systems (formerly Niuera), and Pelican S.A.. Kimera Systems (formerly Niuera) is a former National Geographic and USAID Development Impact Lab awardee.

The project was funded in part by a National Geographic Energy Challenge Grant, a USAID Development Impact Lab Innovation Grant, and an Interamerican Development Bank IDEAS Grant.
